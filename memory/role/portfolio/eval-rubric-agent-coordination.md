# Evaluation Rubric: Education Agent Coordination Protocol RFP

**Created:** 2026-05-02
**For RFP:** rfp-agent-coordination-protocol.md
**Scoring:** 1-5 scale per dimension, weighted. Maximum weighted score: 5.00

---

## Quantitative Scoring (1-5 Scale)

### Technical Merit — 25%

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Protocol architecture is novel, technically rigorous, and clearly builds on A2A+MCP stack. Design handles edge cases (agent failures, conflicting updates, schema evolution). Clear separation of concerns between transport, coordination, and domain semantics. Evidence of prior protocol design experience. |
| 4 | Sound architecture with minor gaps in edge case handling. Builds on A2A+MCP appropriately. Good separation of concerns. Team has relevant systems experience. |
| 3 | Workable architecture but relies on unproven assumptions or hand-waves on key challenges (e.g., conflict resolution, schema versioning). Adequate but not innovative. |
| 2 | Architecture has significant technical risks — unclear how it handles concurrent agent operations, or tightly couples to a single transport layer without abstraction. |
| 1 | Technically unsound or demonstrates misunderstanding of agent coordination challenges. |

### Equity & Learner Impact — 25%

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Equity is structural — embedded in protocol architecture decisions, not just pilot site selection. Informed consent model designed for low digital literacy populations. Participatory design process names specific community partners already engaged. Disaggregated outcomes plan is detailed and credible. Pilot centers the hardest context (rural, low-connectivity, under-resourced). |
| 4 | Strong equity integration with specific target populations and meaningful participatory design plan. Consent model addresses real-world literacy barriers. Minor gaps in disaggregation plan. |
| 3 | Equity is present but separable from the technical work — "we will pilot at a community college" without explaining how the protocol design itself changes to serve that population. Participatory design is mentioned but vague. |
| 2 | Checkbox equity — names "diverse populations" without specificity. Consent model is FERPA-compliant but not learner-centered. No participatory design. Pilot sites are convenient, not intentional. |
| 1 | Equity is absent or an afterthought. Pilot is exclusively at research universities. No consideration of connectivity constraints or digital literacy. |

### Portfolio Fit — 20%

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Directly addresses the identified Agent Orchestration protocol gap. Demonstrates deep understanding of PathWeaver's implementation and TrustLayer's trust framework. Letters of support from existing grantees. Clear plan for compatibility validation. Identifies cross-pillar connections (Local-First, Longitudinal Memory). |
| 4 | Good gap coverage with credible interoperability plan. At least one grantee letter. Understands existing portfolio but may miss some cross-pillar connections. |
| 3 | Addresses the gap but interoperability plan is vague. No grantee letters. Limited understanding of existing portfolio architecture. |
| 2 | Partially addresses the gap but overlaps with PathWeaver or TrustLayer scope. Unclear how it complements rather than duplicates. |
| 1 | Does not address the identified gap, or creates conflicts with existing grants. |

### Team & Execution — 15%

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | PI has published protocol specification work or led open standards efforts. Team covers both distributed systems expertise AND education/credential domain knowledge. No single point of failure — key person risk is mitigated. Institutional support is strong. |
| 4 | Strong PI with relevant track record. Team has most needed expertise; minor gap (e.g., strong on systems, lighter on education domain). Manageable key person risk. |
| 3 | Adequate team but thin in one critical area. PI is credible but hasn't led work at this scope. Some key person risk. |
| 2 | Team is too small for scope, or missing critical expertise (e.g., no one with education credential experience). High key person risk. |
| 1 | Team cannot credibly deliver the proposed work. |

### Budget & Timeline — 10%

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Budget is well-justified, proportional to scope, and within ceiling. Timeline is realistic with appropriate buffer. Milestones are measurable and sequenced logically (compatibility validation before pilot, draft spec before final). |
| 4 | Budget is reasonable with minor justification gaps. Timeline is feasible but tight. |
| 3 | Budget is at ceiling with scope that may exceed capacity. Timeline is ambitious — some milestones may slip. |
| 2 | Budget is poorly justified or scope is mismatched to funding level. Timeline is unrealistic. |
| 1 | Budget request exceeds ceiling, or timeline shows fundamental misunderstanding of the work. |

### Standards Alignment — 5%

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Credible publication pathway through W3C, 1EdTech, or equivalent body. PI has existing relationships with standards community. Plan includes public comment period and community review. |
| 4 | Identified publication pathway with reasonable plan. Some standards community connections. |
| 3 | Intends to publish as open standard but pathway is vague. No existing standards relationships. |
| 2 | Open-source but no standards publication plan. |
| 1 | Proprietary or closed approach. |

## Qualitative Assessment (Intangibles)

Score each as Strong / Adequate / Weak with brief justification:

### Letters of Support Quality
- Are they substantive or boilerplate?
- Do supporting PIs describe specific integration plans, or just general endorsement?
- Are letters from existing portfolio grantees (PathWeaver, TrustLayer, etc.)?

### PI Communication & Responsiveness
- If there was a pre-submission Q&A, how did the PI engage?
- Does the narrative demonstrate clear thinking, or is it padded with jargon?
- Is the writing precise about what they will and won't do?

### Community Reputation
- Is the PI or team known in the agent coordination or education credential space?
- Do they have an active open-source presence?
- Have they been cited or referenced by other researchers in the field?

### Risk Awareness
- Does the proposal honestly name its risks, or does it promise everything will work?
- Are mitigation strategies specific or generic?
- Does the team distinguish between what they know and what they're assuming?

### Institutional Commitment
- Does the host institution provide matching funds, facilities, or personnel?
- Is there evidence of institutional buy-in beyond the PI's lab?
