## Summary

Through building the co-worker prototype and debating what differentiates it from an assistant, we arrived at:

- AI capabilities are table stakes, not differentiators
- The co-worker shouldn't mimic a human colleague — that's anthropomorphizing
- The real innovation is **experiential, not capability-based** — it lives in the interface humans and AI share
- Emergent synergies should come from the **interaction itself**, not from AI doing things humans are bad at
- **Chat is the wrong interface metaphor** — it constrains the experience to feel like an assistant

## Direction

**Shared cognitive workspace** — the work itself as a shared medium, not conversation about the work. Both human and AI operate on shared artifacts with visible contributions.

## What We Ruled Out

1. AI as simulated human colleague (anthropomorphizing)
2. "AI does what humans are bad at" (division of labor = tool pitch)
3. Better memory/data crunching (capabilities, not experience)
4. Industry "co-worker" products (autonomous task execution with a new label)

## Key Research

- CHI 2026: Peer-role AI helped but also disrupted flow — initiative calibration matters
- IDC: "AI agents are instruments, not co-workers" — serious counter-argument
- Socio-cognitive Teammate framework (arXiv): AI models social/cognitive state, not just task
- Colleague Skill (China): Cloning coworkers into AI triggered backlash about identity
- Deloitte: 84% of companies haven't redesigned jobs for AI

## Open Questions

1. What does the AI's "presence" feel like in the workspace?
2. How do contributions flow between human and AI?
3. What's the emergent moment that can't happen in chat or solo work?
4. What does the AI do when the human isn't there?
5. What medium should this live in? (Unknown — should emerge from design)

## Next Step

Continue developing the hypothesis through scenarios, sketches, or further research until the medium and emergent value become clear enough to commit to implementation.
