# Design Exploration: The AI Chief of Staff

**Status:** Exploratory — no decisions made. Ideas only.

## A Day at the Office: The Grant Unwind

---

### Monday morning. Michael hasn't opened his laptop yet.

The Chief of Staff has already been at work. Over the weekend, it reviewed the Q2 reports that came in Friday afternoon. It noticed CredBridge's revised timeline still doesn't address the staffing gap. It noticed EdgeWise's preliminary benchmark numbers leaked into a draft report — latency is still 2.8x off target but trending in the right direction. It noticed LearnTrail hasn't submitted any documentation for the May 30 architecture review, now three weeks away.

It updated the portfolio health dashboard. It drafted a Monday briefing. It flagged LearnTrail as the most urgent item — not because anyone asked, but because that's its job.

---

### 8:15 AM. Michael opens his laptop.

The briefing is waiting. Not in a chat window — in the shared workspace. The portfolio dashboard shows the current state. A note at the top says:

> Three things before your 10am standards call:
> 1. LearnTrail hasn't submitted architecture documentation. May 30 review is 22 days out. Elena may need a nudge — or this is a signal.
> 2. EdgeWise prelim benchmarks show improvement (2.8x → target is 1x). Priya's team is making progress. Full results May 18.
> 3. VP Williams' office called a budget meeting for Thursday. No agenda yet — I'll monitor.

Michael scans it. Thinks: *LearnTrail is getting worse, not better.* He doesn't say anything about it yet. He heads to his standards call.

---

### 11:30 AM. Michael gets out of the standards call.

He checks his email. There's a message from the VP's office: "Need to cut one grant from the portfolio. Budget pressure from the board. Decision needed by end of week."

Michael tells the Chief of Staff: "Patricia wants us to drop a grant. End of week."

The Chief of Staff doesn't ask "which one?" — it already knows the portfolio. It says:

> I've been watching three grants with issues — LearnTrail is the most concerning. Before I put together the full picture, do you want me to assess all three problem grants, or do you already have a sense of direction?

Michael: "Give me all three. But I have a feeling about this."

---

### 11:45 AM. The Chief of Staff works.

It doesn't wait for further instructions. It pulls portfolio state, grantee health records, pillar coverage, budget burn rates, and correspondence history. It runs the analysis across four perspectives — technical viability, equity impact, operational recovery path, and portfolio fit. It drafts a decision brief.

While it works, it notices something Michael hasn't mentioned: if LearnTrail is cut, Provenance drops to two grants, and one of those (CredBridge) is also struggling. It flags this as a structural risk — not a reason not to cut LearnTrail, but something the VP needs to know when the recommendation goes up.

It also checks the calendar. Michael has a 1:1 with VP Williams on the 13th — eight days away. The decision could be socialized there, but the deadline is Friday. It notes this timing tension in the brief.

---

### 12:30 PM. The brief lands in the shared workspace.

Not a chat message. A document — structured, scannable, sitting in the decision records area where Michael can read it, mark it up, push back. The Chief of Staff's analysis is visible. Michael's annotations will be too.

The brief covers all three grants. At the bottom, it says:

> **My assessment:** LearnTrail is the strongest unwind candidate. The risk is structural (lost architect), the recovery path is uncertain, and the 90-day runway we set in April was already a signal. EdgeWise deserves its May 18 benchmark shot. CredBridge is nearly done — unwinding recovers almost nothing.
>
> **What I want to flag:** Cutting LearnTrail weakens the Provenance pillar to two grants, one of which is Yellow. Patricia should know this isn't just a budget line — it's a pillar coverage decision. I'd recommend framing it that way when you bring it to her.

This is different from a Decision Frame. The Chief of Staff has an opinion. It's transparent about the opinion. It's also transparent about what it doesn't know — whether Patricia has other budget considerations, whether Elena has been talking to other funders, whether the board cares about pillar coverage or just the number.

---

### 1:00 PM. Michael reads the brief over lunch.

He agrees with LearnTrail. He marks up one section — he wants the equity impact framed differently for Patricia. He adds a note: "Elena's been a strong partner. I want the wind-down to be respectful. Draft the communication carefully."

The Chief of Staff sees the markup. It doesn't wait for a follow-up instruction — "draft the communication carefully" is enough. It starts drafting the email to Elena, pulling her communication preferences, relationship history, and the tone guidance for struggling grantees. It also starts preparing materials for the Patricia conversation.

---

### 2:30 PM. Two drafts in the workspace.

1. **Email to Dr. Vasquez** — empathetic, specific, referencing the value of her work, proposing a transition call.
2. **Talking points for VP Williams** — framed around pillar coverage impact, not just budget savings. Includes the structural risk to Provenance.

Michael reviews both. He edits one line in the Elena email — makes it more personal. He approves both.

He says: "Send the email to Elena. And put the Patricia materials in my prep for the 13th."

---

### 2:45 PM. The Chief of Staff executes.

It sends the email. It logs the communication in Elena's contact file and the correspondence record. It updates the LearnTrail grantee status to "Wind-down initiated." It adds the Patricia talking points to the May 13 meeting prep. It creates a task: "Follow up on Elena's response — expect reply within 48 hours."

Michael didn't ask for any of those follow-on actions. The Chief of Staff did them because that's what a chief of staff does — they don't just send the email, they close the loop.

---

### Wednesday, 9:00 AM. Michael opens his laptop.

The briefing says:

> Elena replied yesterday evening. She's requesting a call Wednesday at 3pm CT to discuss the transition. She specifically wants to talk about preserving the provenance graph research. I checked your calendar — 3pm is open. Should I set up the meeting?

Michael: "Yes."

The Chief of Staff creates the Google Meet event, sends invites to Elena and Michael, replies to Elena confirming the time and sharing the Meet link, updates the calendar, and starts a prep doc for the 3pm call — pulling Elena's communication style, the relationship history, the key points from the wind-down decision, and notes on what "preserving the research" might look like operationally.

Michael gets a quiet notification: "Meeting set for 3pm. Prep doc is in the workspace when you're ready."

---

### Wednesday, 3:45 PM. The call is over.

Michael tells the Chief of Staff what happened: "Elena took it well. She wants to open-source the provenance graph data model. She asked about a no-cost extension for 60 days to clean up documentation. I told her I'd consider it."

The Chief of Staff captures the debrief. It creates a decision record for the no-cost extension request. It updates Elena's contact profile. It drafts a follow-up email to Elena summarizing the agreed next steps. It flags the no-cost extension as a decision that needs Patricia's sign-off and adds it to the May 13 prep.

It also adds a note to the pattern library: "When unwinding a grant with a strong PI, offer a documentation wind-down period. It preserves institutional value and maintains the relationship."

Michael didn't ask for any of that. He just talked about his day.

---

## What's different from the current co-worker?

The current co-worker could do most of these *tasks* if asked. The difference is in **who's driving.** In this scenario, Michael made three decisions: which grant to cut, to send the email, and to take the call. Everything else — the monitoring, the analysis, the drafting, the follow-through, the pattern capture — was the Chief of Staff doing its job without being asked.

The human brings judgment, relationships, and authority. The Chief of Staff brings continuity, thoroughness, and operational follow-through. Neither is waiting for the other to tell them what to do next.
